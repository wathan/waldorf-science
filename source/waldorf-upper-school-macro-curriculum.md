# A Macro-Curriculum for Waldorf High School (Grades 9–12)

## Status and sourcing

This is a **working synthesis**, assembled for the purpose of evaluating and justifying meso-level
content decisions (e.g. course design, PISA-competency alignment). It is **not** a transcription of
any single authoritative document — no such grade-by-grade, content-free macro-curriculum for
the upper school appears to be openly published. It functions as an *ideal type* in Bransby &
Rawson's sense: offered as orientation, expected to be modified through use, and superseded if a
more authoritative source (e.g. the unpublished upper-school volume referenced in Bransby &
Rawson 2021/2022) becomes available.

**Primary skeleton:** Douglas Gerwin, *"Four Phases of Teenage Development Reflected in the
Waldorf High School Curriculum"* (Waldorf Publications, 2015) — the What/How/Why/Who and
observation/comparison/analysis/synthesis sequence, plus its embedded body–self–world
developmental narrative and explicit spiral-curriculum framing.

**Triangulated against:**
- The ICSWE/Pedagogical Section *Vertical Curriculum* (physics, chemistry, natural science,
  geography sections) — for "structure and transformation" (g.9), "polarity and intensification"
  (g.10), "meta-conceptual competence" and individuation (g.11), and "comprehensive synthesis"
  (g.12).
- Bransby & Rawson, *Waldorf Education for the Future* (2021) — for the body/self/other/world
  relational axis, the twelve potentialities, and the goodness→beauty→truth disposition
  sequence (adolescence = "the question for truth").
- Cross-school convergence (Emerson, Kimberton, Denver, San Francisco Waldorf) — confirms the
  four-term skeleton while revealing a genuine soft boundary between grades 11 and 12 (see
  Cross-cutting notes).

## Overarching journey

If adolescence as a whole is governed by "the question for truth," the four upper-school years
can be read as four successive angles of approach to that question — each one reorganising a
different relation between the young person and the world:

| Grade | Question | Capacity | Relation reorganised |
|---|---|---|---|
| 9  | What? | Observation | Body ↔ World (the given) |
| 10 | How?  | Comparison  | Self ↔ System (the related) |
| 11 | Why?  | Analysis    | Self-as-knower ↔ World's constituents (the individual/invisible) |
| 12 | Who?  | Synthesis   | Self ↔ Whole-world, consciously held (the integrated) |

The sequence is also spiral: grade 12 does not introduce a new orientation so much as
consciously recapitulate grade 1's unreflective sense of a whole world — "the image of the
whole," now known "for the first time."

## Potentialities: vertical development across grades 9–12

Potentialities are not assigned to individual grades; they are lifelong capacities exercised
throughout all four years. What changes across grades is the *mode* of exercise — the
developmental form in which each potentiality is active. The table below shows this vertical
development for the potentialities most centrally at stake in the upper school. It should be
read alongside the grade-by-grade descriptions below, not instead of them.

| Potentiality | Grade 9 mode | Grade 10 mode | Grade 11 mode | Grade 12 mode |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Senses** | Sharpened sensory attention; trusting direct perception as the starting point for all knowing | Comparative perception — noticing how phenomena differ from and relate to each other | Inferential sensing — attending to the *effects* of what cannot be directly perceived | Integrative perception — recognising the whole through its sensory parts |
| **Imagination** | Exact, concrete imagination: vivid description of what is directly observed | Relational imagination: picturing the dynamic interaction between opposing poles | Theoretical imagination: forming coherent mental models of invisible entities | Holistic imagination: holding the whole — the planet, the species, the epoch — as a single image |
| **Enquiry** | Descriptive: "What is actually here?" — questions that open phenomena for exact observation | Relational: "How do these connect?" — questions about interaction, polarity and transformation | Analytical/hypothetical: "Why is it this way?" — questions that require models of the unobservable | Synthetic: "Who or what stands behind this whole?" — questions that integrate previously separate enquiries |
| **Holistic thinking** | Implicit: experienced as the world's given wholeness, not yet consciously grasped | Emerging: grasping how opposing elements belong to a single dynamic system | Analytical: temporarily dissolving the whole into its individual constituents — a necessary step toward conscious synthesis | Explicit: consciously re-integrating the analysed parts into a coherent, responsible whole |
| **Judgement** | Descriptive judgement: "Is this observation accurate and complete?" | Comparative judgement: "Which interpretation best fits the relationship between these phenomena?" | Analytical/evaluative: "Which model best explains the evidence — and what are the limits of this model?" | Synthetic/ethical: "What does this whole situation call for? What is my responsibility within it?" |
| **Health and wellbeing** | Central and acute: the newly matured body is itself the developmental challenge | Stabilising: the search for equilibrium, a personal fulcrum between bodily and cognitive life | Background: a stable bodily foundation enables inward, theoretical work | Integrative: physical, cognitive and social wellbeing held together as aspects of a single biographical whole |
| **Future thinking** | Near-term biographical: "What is happening to me, right now?" | Medium-term: beginning to locate oneself within systems and social patterns that extend beyond the immediate | Biographical individuation: "This is my own course — I must find and take it" | Long-range: "What is my responsibility toward the world I am about to enter?" |
| **Democratic participation** | Largely implicit: learning to live with the tensions of a pluralistic community | Relational: recognising that opposing positions can belong to a larger productive whole | Critical: evaluating whose voice is heard, whose model is privileged, and why | Active: taking up a considered position within a community and contributing to its direction |

*Note: Language and communication, Empathy, Aesthetics, and Lifelong learning are also
exercised throughout; their vertical development is not shown here because they are less
grade-differentiated in character and are better addressed at the micro-curriculum level in
relation to specific activities and subject disciplines.*

## Grade 9

- **Guiding question:** *What?* — What is here? What happened? What is actually going on?

- **Trained capacity:** Observation — exact, theory-free description of phenomena as directly
  given, prior to explanation. The *Senses* potentiality is the primary vehicle: sensory
  attention is deliberately sharpened as a cognitive act, not merely treated as a
  pre-theoretical given. Confidence in one's own perception — the foundational epistemic
  stance of grade 9 — is itself a cultivated capacity, not an assumption.

- **Developmental context:** The onset of puberty brings new intensity to bodily experience
  just as abstract thought begins to awaken. The tension between materiality and abstraction
  is experienced as turbulence, sometimes as struggle or revolt. The *Health and wellbeing*
  potentiality is acutely at stake: the body has become unfamiliar, and the year's work
  proceeds from the premise that re-establishing a trustworthy relationship to one's own body
  and to the directly encountered world is the precondition for all further cognitive
  development.

- **Relational shift:** Body ↔ World. The young person's relationship to their own body has
  itself become unfamiliar; the task is to re-establish a trustworthy relationship to the world
  as immediately encountered, body included. The *Imagination* potentiality operates in its
  most concrete mode: exact, vivid description of what is actually perceived — not yet
  hypothesis, not yet theory.

- **Epistemic stance:** Direct perception is taken at face value. Phenomena are met "as they
  are" — described and recorded before being theorised. *Enquiry* in grade 9 takes a
  descriptive form: the question is always "What is actually here?" rather than "Why is it
  this way?" *Judgement* operates at the level of accuracy: "Is this observation complete and
  honest?"

- **Cross-curricular motif:** Unresolved opposition. Phenomena present as pairs of contraries
  not yet reconciled — heat/cold, expansion/contraction, revolution/old order, colliding
  tectonic plates, structure revealed through contrast. The opposition is experienced and
  described, not yet resolved — resolution belongs to grade 10.

- **Spiral pairing:** Continues and intensifies grade 8's turn toward a technological/
  mechanical grasp of the world, now inflected by the bodily upheaval of puberty.

## Grade 10

- **Guiding question:** *How?* — How does this relate to that? How did opposing things come to
  interact, and what do they produce together?

- **Trained capacity:** Comparison — weighing contrasting phenomena against one another to
  find their relative value, origin, and significance. *Holistic thinking* emerges in a
  specific form here: not yet the capacity to synthesize a whole consciously (that belongs to
  grade 12), but the capacity to grasp how opposing elements belong to a single dynamic
  system. The *Aesthetics* potentiality is particularly active: the sense of balance, form
  and proportion — of things fitting together — is both a felt experience and a cognitive
  tool.

- **Developmental context:** The bodily turbulence of grade 9 settles into a new physical and
  psychological equilibrium; the search is for a personal "fulcrum" or midpoint between
  extremes. *Health and wellbeing* stabilises: bodily and cognitive life are no longer in
  open conflict, and this equilibrium creates the conditions for comparative and relational
  thinking to emerge. *Future thinking* begins to extend beyond the immediate: the student
  starts locating themselves within patterns and systems that reach beyond their personal
  situation.

- **Relational shift:** Self ↔ System. Rather than facing isolated given phenomena, the self
  begins locating itself within systems of interacting, often opposing, forces and elements.
  *Imagination* becomes relational: the student can now picture the dynamic interaction
  between opposing poles, rather than only describing each pole separately. *Empathy*
  operates epistemologically here: the comparative stance toward phenomena — genuinely
  entering into each pole before judging between them — is a form of epistemic empathy.

- **Epistemic stance:** Reasoning becomes relational and polar — two contrasting terms are
  understood as poles of a single system whose interaction generates something new
  (intensification). *Enquiry* takes a relational form: "How do these connect?" *Judgement*
  becomes comparative: "Which interpretation best fits the relationship between these
  phenomena?" Confidence grows that one's own thinking can grasp *how things connect*, not
  just *what they are*.

- **Cross-curricular motif:** Polarity and intensification. Opposed terms — acid/base,
  centripetal/centrifugal, high-pressure/low-pressure systems — combine to produce new
  forms or states. The resolution of grade 9's unresolved oppositions arrives here, not as
  the elimination of one pole but as the recognition that both belong to a generative
  tension.

- **Spiral pairing:** Prepares the ground for grade 11 by establishing the systems and wholes
  within which grade 11's individual constituent elements will later be located and analysed.

## Grade 11

- **Guiding question:** *Why?* — Why are things this way? At its deepest reach this extends
  into questions of destiny, meaning, and responsibility — where it begins to shade into
  "Who?" (see Cross-cutting notes).

- **Trained capacity:** Analysis — independent, abstract theorising; resolving a whole into
  its individual constituent elements. *Imagination* now operates in its theoretical mode:
  the student forms coherent mental models of entities that cannot be directly perceived (the
  atom, the electrical field, the cell as a cosmological unit). This is not fantasy but
  disciplined theoretical construction — and it is only available because exact observation
  (grade 9) and relational thinking (grade 10) have already established the experiential
  ground from which models depart.

- **Developmental context:** A new depth and individuality of inner life brings an urge to
  break from what has been given — by family, peers, or prior schooling — and set out on
  one's own course. *Future thinking* takes its biographical form: "This is my own course —
  I must find and take it." *Democratic participation* becomes critical: the student begins
  evaluating whose voice is heard in the communities and disciplines they are part of, and
  why. *Health and wellbeing* recedes to the background as a stable platform; this stability
  is what makes the inward, theoretical work of the year possible.

- **Relational shift:** Self-as-knower ↔ World's constituents. Two movements happen together:
  outward, toward the smallest or most fundamental units of a previously-grasped whole (the
  element, the atom, the cell, the individual historical actor); and inward, toward the self
  as the one constructing the models used to grasp them.

- **Epistemic stance:** Meta-conceptual competence. Thinking is no longer anchored only in
  what the senses give directly; models of the unobservable become both accessible and
  trustworthy — and are recognised as *constructions*, not direct transcriptions of reality.
  *Enquiry* takes its hypothetical/analytical form: "Why is it this way?" — requiring the
  formation and evaluation of explanatory models. *Judgement* becomes evaluative and
  comparative across models: "Which account best fits the evidence, and what are the limits
  of this model?" *Senses* operate inferentially: attention is now directed toward the
  *effects* of what cannot be directly perceived, rather than toward directly available
  phenomena.

- **Cross-curricular motif:** The invisible / the individual element. Atomic structure,
  electricity, cell theory, individual entries of the periodic table, the solitary quester of
  Grail or Renaissance narratives, the point at infinity in projective geometry.

- **Spiral pairing:** Returns to a theme first met concretely and practically in grade 7, now
  treated abstractly and theoretically.

## Grade 12

- **Guiding question:** *Who?* — Who is this being called human? Who, or what, stands behind
  the whole? Pursued far enough, this becomes "Why?" (see Cross-cutting notes).

- **Trained capacity:** Synthesis — drawing previously-analysed, disparate elements and
  disciplines back into a coherent whole. *Holistic thinking* reaches its explicit, conscious
  form: the student actively constructs coherent wholes from the separate parts analysed in
  grade 11, rather than simply experiencing wholeness unreflectively (as in early childhood)
  or grasping systemic relations (as in grade 10). *Imagination* operates at its most
  encompassing: holding the planet, the species, the epoch — or the self's entire
  biographical trajectory — as a single image.

- **Developmental context:** Approaching full physical and cognitive maturity, the young person
  becomes capable of a panoramic, retrospective view across their own education and
  development. *Future thinking* extends to the long-range and the ethical: "What is my
  responsibility toward the world I am about to enter?" *Democratic participation* becomes
  active: taking up a considered position within a community and contributing to its
  direction, not merely critiquing it. *Lifelong learning* comes into view as a conscious
  orientation: the student begins to understand their own education as a starting point
  rather than a completion.

- **Relational shift:** Self ↔ Whole-world, consciously held. A sense of wholeness returns
  (cf. grade 1's unreflective participation in a whole world), but now achieved through
  conscious synthesis — with the self located within and responsible toward that whole.
  *Health and wellbeing* is held alongside cognitive and social dimensions as aspects of a
  single biographical whole, no longer compartmentalised.

- **Epistemic stance:** Synthetic and holistic judgement. Multiple individuated elements and
  models (including those constructed in grade 11) are integrated into a coherent picture,
  and that integration is recognised as the student's own achievement. *Enquiry* asks its
  most comprehensive question: "Who or what stands behind this whole?" *Judgement* becomes
  synthetic and ethical: "What does this whole situation call for? What is my responsibility
  within it?" *Senses* become integrative: the whole is perceived through the careful
  attention to its parts that earlier years developed.

- **Cross-curricular motif:** Comprehensive synthesis. World history, a survey of
  architecture, the unity of the animal kingdom culminating in the human being, biochemistry
  reuniting the elements analysed in grade 11.

- **Spiral pairing:** Recapitulates grade 1 — "the image of the whole" — but now known
  consciously, "for the first time."

## Cross-cutting notes

**The grade 11/12 "Why"/"Who" boundary is soft, not an error.** Gerwin's own text glosses
grade 11's deepest "Why" questions as questions of "destiny, life's meaning, social
responsibility," and grade 12's "Who" as asking who or what "stands behind the outer play of
events... pulling them together into a synthesizing whole." At their furthest extension these
converge on the same question asked two ways. Schools that place "Who" in grade 11 (e.g. San
Francisco Waldorf) and "Why" in grade 12 are emphasising the same convergence from the other
side. Treat content that sits at this boundary (e.g. material concerning agency, responsibility,
or "who is behind this") as legitimately placeable in either year rather than as evidence of
misclassification.

**Potentialities develop continuously, not in steps.** The vertical-development table above
shows a clean four-stage progression for analytical purposes, but in practice the modes
overlap and are not sharply separated. A grade 9 student will exercise some analytical
Enquiry; a grade 12 student will still rely on exact sensory observation. What the table
captures is a shift in which mode is *most centrally at stake* at each grade — the
predominant developmental challenge — not an on/off switching of capacities.

**Spiral pairings are illustrative, not a complete system.** Gerwin offers grade 7↔11 and
grade 8↔9 only "for example." The grade 12↔1 pairing is the one he develops explicitly and
at length. No claim should be built on an assumed pairing for grade 10 beyond its
forward-preparatory relationship to grade 11.

**Using this document for meso-level design.** For any candidate piece of content (a course,
a unit, a reorganisation of existing material), apply the diagnostic from Bransby & Rawson:
*if this content is the pedagogical answer to this grade's developmental question, what is
that question, and how good an answer is it?* Concretely:

1. Identify which grade's *relational shift* and *epistemic stance* the content most
   plausibly serves.
2. Check whether the content exercises the potentialities in the *mode* appropriate to that
   grade — e.g. content that exercises Enquiry in its descriptive mode ("What is here?")
   fits grade 9; content that exercises Enquiry in its hypothetical mode ("Why is it this
   way?") fits grade 11.
3. If the content's natural fit is to a different grade than its current placement, that is
   worth knowing — but not necessarily a problem. Some content performs the macro-level
   function of one grade while being timetabled in another; this is itself a finding worth
   reporting.
4. Be alert to content that exercises potentialities in an earlier mode than the grade
   warrants (e.g. purely descriptive Enquiry in grade 11) — this may indicate
   under-challenge — and to content that skips earlier modes (e.g. hypothetical Enquiry
   without the observational foundation of grades 9–10) — this may indicate missing
   preconditions.

**Portability.** The *relational shifts*, *epistemic stances*, *trained capacities*, and
*potentiality modes* are the candidate macro-level content — the parts intended to be
culturally portable. The *cross-curricular motifs* and specific examples under *developmental
context* are Gerwin's (or the Vertical Curriculum's) meso-level instantiations and carry no
claim to portability. When adapting this framework to a new context, the motifs and examples
are exactly what should be expected to change; the rows above them are the working hypothesis
being tested.

## Glossary

**Cross-curricular motif** — The shared thematic content (e.g. "polarity and intensification,"
"the invisible") through which a grade's developmental theme is independently instantiated
across separate subjects. This is meso-level: the motif is one possible answer to a macro-level
question, and is expected to vary by tradition and culture (see *Portability*, above).

**Developmental task** — What children and young people need to engage with, at a given stage,
in order to learn and develop. Arises partly from the innate nature of the developing human
being and partly from the requirements of the surrounding society and culture (Rawson 2020;
Bransby & Rawson 2021).

**Epistemic stance** — The characteristic relationship to knowledge and explanatory models
available to students at a given grade — e.g. trusting direct perception (grade 9) versus
trusting models of the unobservable (grade 11). Distinct from *cross-curricular motif*: the
stance is the macro-level capacity; the motif is the meso-level vehicle through which it is
exercised.

**Generative principle** — A content-free pedagogical claim (e.g. "learning is a rhythmical
process," "direct experience is the basis for learning") drawn from Steiner's pedagogical
anthropology and elaborated by Rawson (2021). Generative principles apply across all grades and
subjects and sit at the *meta-level*.

**Horizontal structuring** — One of three axes (with *vertical* and *spiral*) along which a
macro-curriculum is organised: the linking of different subjects within the same year around a
shared theme — e.g. grade 9's "unresolved opposition" appearing in physics, chemistry, history,
and geography alike.

**Ideal type** — A model offered as a "best fit" orientation for a developmental task, based on
evidence and experience, expected to be modified by practitioners and to evolve through use
(Weber's term, adopted by Rawson). This document is itself offered as an ideal type, not a fixed
prescription.

**Macro-curriculum / macro level** — The level at which curriculum is described purely in terms
of age-sensitive developmental themes — how learners relate to themselves, their bodies, others,
and the world — with no specific content. Structured horizontally, vertically, and spirally
(Bransby & Rawson 2021).

**Meso-curriculum / meso level** — The level at which a particular culture, country, or school
selects specific content, topics, and methods as a "possible answer" to the developmental
questions posed at the macro level. Content lives here, not at the macro level.

**Meta-conceptual competence** — The capacity, said to emerge around grade 11, to hold an
explanatory model (e.g. of the atom) as a constructed way of looking at something, rather than
as a direct description of reality — i.e. awareness of one's own role in constructing the model
(ICSWE Vertical Curriculum, physics/chemistry).

**Meta-level** — The level above macro/meso/micro comprising the basic generative principles and
aims of the education as a whole, derived from an anthroposophical understanding of the
developing human being (Bransby & Rawson 2021).

**Micro-curriculum / micro level** — The level at which an individual teacher plans and delivers
lessons for a specific class, drawing on both the macro-level themes and the meso-level content
available in their context.

**Phenomenological method** — The pedagogical approach, used especially in physics and chemistry
through grade 10, in which experiment and direct observation precede and ground theoretical
explanation, rather than illustrating a theory given in advance.

**Potentiality** — One of twelve content-free, lifelong human capacities identified by Bransby &
Rawson (2021) as what curriculum, at every level, ultimately develops. Potentialities are not
grade-assigned; they are exercised across all four years in developmentally distinct modes (see
vertical-development table).

**Potentiality mode** — The developmentally specific form in which a potentiality is exercised
at a given grade. The same potentiality (e.g. Enquiry) is present throughout all four years but
operates descriptively in grade 9, relationally in grade 10, hypothetically/analytically in
grade 11, and synthetically in grade 12.

**Relational shift** — The change, specific to each grade, in how the young person relates to
their own body, self, others, and the world. The defining content of the macro level, per
Bransby & Rawson (2021).

**Spiral structuring** — One of three axes (with *horizontal* and *vertical*) along which a
macro-curriculum is organised: the recurrence of a theme at increasing depth across the school
career, such that a later grade revisits an earlier one "at a higher level" (Gerwin's
tower/spiral-staircase image; grade 12 recapitulating grade 1).

**"The question for truth"** — Bransby & Rawson's (2021) characterisation of the single
disposition underlying all four upper-school years collectively, as the adolescent counterpart
to "the world is good" (early childhood) and "the world is beautiful" (primary years). The four
grade-specific questions (What/How/Why/Who) can be read as four angles on this single question.

**Vertical structuring** — One of three axes (with *horizontal* and *spiral*) along which a
macro-curriculum is organised: the progression of a single capacity or skill set across the
whole school career — e.g. observation → comparison → analysis → synthesis, grades 9–12; or
descriptive → relational → hypothetical → synthetic Enquiry, grades 9–12.
